A Step Closer to Defeating COVID-19: The Importance of Vaccines

Introduction:

    The WHO (World Health Organization) declared COVID-19 as a worldwide pandemic on the 11th of March in 2020, because of the speed and scale at which the disease was spreading. Prior to that, it was first reported in Wuhan, a city in China, on February 26. As early studies began, the infectious substance of COVID-19 was discovered to be a novel coronavirus, but as more studies were practiced, the virus was found to be related to the SARS outbreak in the early 2000s. According to the WHO, over 8,000 people were infected by this outbreak and over 750 people died. Therefore, the International Committee for Taxonomy of Viruses named this new virus, SARS-CoV-2 (severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus-2), commonly known as COVID-19.

    The origin of this very dangerous disease is still not fully verified yet, although cases have been connected to a Huanan seafood market where wild animals like bats, birds and snakes are sold. An online article, published by the National Center for Biotechnology, showed a study that was conducted in the early stages of the pandemic which suggested a bat to be the origin of the virus. Another theory of the origin suggested the origin was a Pangolin, but evidence of transmission from human to human became the most supported theory after a visit by the World Health Organization on January 22, 2020. Since the outbreak in February 2020 in just 4 short months there were over eight thousand cases of COVID-19, a number that only continues to exponentially increase as more time passes. As of the beginning of this month (December 2021) their has been over 271 million cases of COVID-19, with over 50 million cases stemming from the US.

How it Spreads/How to Prevent:

    COVID-19 can be transmitted through people by breathing, talking, coughing, or sneezing near or in close contact with diseased patients. After an analysis, done by the CDC, of trend and mitigation measures in Wuhan, Italy, and New York City, it was shown that airborne transmission is the dominant way the disease spreads. This analysis revealed that among the many mitigations measures the importance of wearing a mask is very dominant in the formation of the trends we are seeing when talking about the spread of the disease. Even though, 80% of individuals are asymptomatic, due to good immune response, there is still sufficient evidence that these individuals can indeed infect others with the disease. The most efficient way to avoid the spread of the virus is by taking various precautions given by the CDC. These precautions include washing your hands frequently, practicing social distances with others, covering your nose and mouth with a mask and cleaning/disinfecting surfaces touched frequently.


    Wearing masks is extremely important to prevent the transmission of COVID-19. As stated earlier, the virus spreads from one person to another who are in close contact with one another, by the transmission of little balls of saliva and moisture when a COVID-19 patient either talks, sneezes or coughs.

Symptoms:

    When you contract COVID-19 you can experience a very wide range of reported symptoms, most symptoms appear anywhere from two days to two weeks after you’ve been exposed to the virus. According to the CDC, the following are symptoms that may conclude that you have COVID-19:

  • Fever or Chills
  • Cough
  • Short of Breadth
  • Diffculty Breathing
  • Diarrhea
  • Fatigue
  • Cough
  • Muscle Ache
  • Body Ache
  • Headache
  • Loss of Smell
  • Loss of Taste
  • Sore Throat
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting

    •     Even though many of these symptoms do overlap with other less serious diseases, like the flu or a cold, it is of major importance to get yourself tested if you have been in close contact with someone that has contracted the disease of if you are showing any of the symptoms above.

    Quarintine/Isolation:

        It is very important to quarantine if you have been within six feet of someone with COVID-19 unless you have been fully vaccinated. If you are fully vaccinated, you do not need to quarantine unless they were experiencing the symptoms described above. It is still a good idea for vaccinated people to get tested anywhere between five to seven days after exposure, even if they experience no symptoms and they should also wear a mask while indoors for the following 14 days after the exposure or until they receive a negative test result. After your quarantine is completed, it is still important to watch your symptoms until 14 days after exposure, if you do happen to feel symptoms it is important to immediately self-isolate and to make sure you contact your local health provider.


        Isolation is also very important to keeping the spread of the disease very low. Isolation is used to divide those who are infected and those who are not. If you are in isolation you should stay at home until it is safe, at home everyone that has COVID-19 should be separated. While in isolation it is important to monitor your symptoms, also try to use separate bathrooms with those who have the virus, if possible. You should make sure to avoid contact with all other members of the house, including pets and weak a mask when or if you need to be around others. Isolation should be fourteen days long, it is very important to isolate for all fourteen days because even though you may not be experiencing any symptoms, you could still transmit the disease.

    Vaccines:

        Getting vaccinated is the best way to prevent the spread of COVID-19. The CDC has approved the distribution and administration of three different vaccines. The three vaccines include, Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna, and Johnson & Johnson’s Janssen. Although all three of the vaccinations are safe and effective in reducing the risk of getting the disease each vaccine is different and it’s important to understand these differences when choosing which vaccine to get.


    Pfizer-BioNTech:

        The Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine is an mRNA vaccine that requires two separate shots 21 days apart, both are injected in the upper part of your arm. This vaccine is recommended for people ages five or older. There is also a new booster shot recommended for those who meet the age requirement, 18 years or older and are at a higher risk of COVID-19. This booster is recommended to be applied six months after you get the first two doses. Nearly all the ingredients found in the vaccine can be found in many foods like fat, salts, and sugars and a harmless piece of mRNA. This mRNA teaches your body to synthetize the immune response which allows it to defend against the virus in the future. Once the immunity is created your body discards all other ingredients in the vaccine, the same way your body discards any substance that it doesn’t need anymore. It is important to note that there are two slightly different Pfizer-BioNTech vaccines, one for ages twelve and older and one for ages five – eleven.

    Ingredients Not Used:
  • No Preservatives
  • No Antibiotics
  • No Medicines
  • No Therapeutics
  • No Tissues
  • No Food Proteins
  • No Metals
  • No Latex

    • Possible Side Effects:
    In the arm where you got the shot:
  • Pain
  • Redness
  • Swelling
    • Throughout the rest of your body:
  • Tiredness
  • Headache
  • Muscle pain
  • Chills
  • Fever
  • Nausea

    •     Most side effects occur within seven days of getting vaccinated and are mostly mild but still common, there is a much higher probability of experiencing these side effects after you received your second dose. Based on evidence from clinical trials conducted by FDA (Food and Drug Administration), the vaccine was successful at halting the spread of the disease at a 95% efficiency rate for ages 16 or older. For ages 5-15, the trials concluded that the vaccine had a greater that 90% efficacy in preventing the disease. The CDC is working very closely with the FDA to ensure that more updates will be provided as more research is being made on the vaccine.


    Moderna:

        The Moderna vaccine is also an mRNA vaccine that requires two separate shots 28 days apart. Just like the previous vaccine, this one is also given on the upper arm and is approved for those who are 18 years or older. Once again, there is also a recommended booster shot that should be taken six months after the second dosage. All ingredients in this vaccine can be commonly found in many foods and includes a mRNA that teaches the cells to be immune.

    •     In clinical trials, there were signs of side effects within seven days of receiving the vaccine. According to the CDC, after the second dose there was a significant increase of side effects throughout the rest of your body. Also, based on these medical studies, this vaccine was effective against 94% of the time against COVID-19. It is important to note that this is only effective if you have received both parts of the vaccine. Another significant discovery in the trials was an increased effectiveness against COVID-19 among people with diverse age, race, sex, and ethnic groups.

    Johnson & Johnson Janssen:

        The Johnson & Johnson Janssen vaccine is also given on the upper arm muscle, but it is not an mRNA vaccine, it is a viral vector. According to the CDC, this vaccine uses “a modified version of a virus that is different from the virus being targeted to deliver important instructions to our cells. The modified version of the virus is called a vector virus.” Also, according to the CDC, as the vector enters the body, it creates a benign spike protein. Your immune system then recognizes this protein as an intruder and triggers the production of antibodies and activates cells to fight off this protein. This is how our bodies learn to defend our bodies from any viruses in the future, like COVID-19. It is of importance to note that the J&J vaccine only requires one dose and is approved to be administered to those who are 18 years or older.

    Ingredients Not Used:
  • No Preservatives
  • No Eggs
  • No Metals
  • No Latex

    •     In clinical trials, side effects can occur within one or two days after getting administered the vaccine. These are not necessarily bad, as these can be signs that the vaccine is doing its job teaching your body to protect it against future viruses. These symptoms should no longer be visible after a few days. In the medical trials the J&J vaccine was proven to be effective 66.3% of the time against COVID-19. One important thing to note for this vaccine is that this vaccine did have a significantly higher rate at preventing hospitalization or death.

    Myths on Vaccines:

        There are many misconceptions about vaccines in general and more specifically about COVID-19 vaccines. Below I will debunk a few of the many myths:

           1) Can the COVID-19 vaccines cause variants?

    a) No, new variants are created by natural changes through a natural mutation. COVID-19 vaccines help prevent new variants from emerging, a high vaccination population helps reduce the spread of the virus and prevents the emergence of new variants

           2) Can receiving a COVID-19 vaccine make you magnetic?

    a) No, COVID-19 vaccines do not contain any ingredients that can create an electromagnetic field. Every available COVID-19 vaccine is free of any metals. Once your body learns to be immune it will get rid of all unused materials into waste.

           3) Will the COVID-19 vaccine alter my DNA?

    a) No, COVID-19 does not change or interact with your DNA. Both mRNA and viral vector vaccines deliver their instructions to the cells to begin building the protection against the virus. The material never actually enters the nucleus, which is where our DNA is kept.

           4) Can I get a COVID-19 vaccine if I would like to have a baby one day?

    a) Yes, a COVID-19 vaccination is recommended for anyone over the age of five years, even for those who are looking to get pregnant in the near future. Currently, there is no evident that shows COVID-19 cause infertility

    Where to get Vaccinated:

        Getting a vaccine is actually very simple, they are available at almost all major pharmacies, hospitals and there are even pop-up shops dedicated for the distribution of these vaccines. A very useful website to find vaccine locations near you is vaccines.gov. This site is very user friendly; you just need to know you zip code and select what vaccine you are looking to get. Once you enter the search you will get several places near you that offer that vaccine.
